Disco Elysium is a role-playing game unlike anything else. It begins with a man waking up on the floor of a trashed hostel room, his memory obliterated, his body in shambles, and his internal monologue screaming. He has no idea who he is, what he’s done, or why there’s a dead man hanging behind the building.

You are that man—a detective, barely functional, physically and mentally ruined. And yet, you have a job to do. You must investigate the murder, navigate the ruins of your fractured identity, and confront the broken city around you. All of this unfolds in the war-torn coastal district of Martinaise, a place soaked in post-revolution disillusionment, where flags of failed ideologies still hang limp in the cold breeze.

What makes Disco Elysium so singular isn’t just the premise—it’s how it lets you engage with it. Instead of traditional RPG combat, the game is built around conversation, introspection, and thought. Your mind is a battlefield. Dozens of skills, each personified as characters inside your head, argue and whisper and shout over each other as you try to make sense of the world. Empathy might urge you to comfort a crying child. Electrochemistry might push you to score speed behind a convenience store. Logic might tell you to keep quiet, while Drama encourages you to lie spectacularly.

You aren’t building a character—you are unearthing one. Every decision you make shapes who this detective becomes. Will you chase redemption, or spiral further into self-destruction? Will you uncover the truth, or cling to comforting lies? Will you embrace ideology, reject it, parody it, or become its vessel? The game never forces a direction. It simply lets you fall—or rise—as you will.

The city of Revachol is more than a backdrop. It’s a living, bleeding character. A place soaked in history, pain, and poetry. The people who live there—from union bosses to working-class mothers to cryptozoologists and teenage punks—are all navigating the aftermath of a world that promised change but delivered collapse. Every conversation you have, every place you visit, deepens the sense that this is not a world built for heroes. It’s a world that keeps moving whether you show up or not.

And yet, Disco Elysium is often hilarious. It’s absurd, surreal, and full of strange beauty. It allows for moments of tenderness, quiet melancholy, and poetic madness. One minute you’re debating dialectical materialism with your subconscious, the next you’re having an emotional breakdown over your tie. Somehow, it all fits. Somehow, it all matters.

The art direction is stunning—painterly and expressive, like a political cartoon sketched over oil on canvas. The soundtrack, composed by British Sea Power, is ethereal, mournful, and strangely triumphant. It pulses through scenes like a heartbeat beneath the narrative’s skin.

Disco Elysium doesn’t ask you to save the world. It doesn’t even ask you to solve the case particularly well. It simply asks: who are you? And how do you live with the answer?

It is a game that trusts you to think, to reflect, and to listen—not just to others, but to yourself. There’s no easy resolution, no ultimate reward, no perfect ending. But there is meaning, if you’re willing to dig deep enough to find it.

Few games aim this high. Fewer still actually reach it. Disco Elysium does, and it doesn’t even break a sweat.

You wake up in a cheap hostel room in Martinaise, Revachol, after what appears to be one of the worst hangovers in human history. You don’t know your name. You don’t know what city you’re in. You don’t know what you’ve done. Your necktie is trying to kill you, your reflection fills you with existential horror, and your body feels like it was dropped from a great height. A voice in your head tells you you've done something irreversible.

Then someone knocks on the door. A man in a neat blue jacket introduces himself as Lieutenant Kim Kitsuragi. He tells you you’re a police officer. There’s a body hanging from a tree behind the building—has been for a week now—and you were supposed to investigate it. The investigation hasn’t started. Your badge and gun are missing. You don’t remember where you live. Or why you're here. Or who you even are.

And so begins your journey—not just to solve a murder, but to reconstruct a broken self. As you follow leads, interview suspects, and uncover layers of corruption and revolution, you slowly begin to piece together your identity. Your name is Harrier Du Bois, and you're not just a detective—you’re the detective. Or you were, once. You were brilliant, passionate, maybe even heroic. But somewhere along the way, you broke. Something inside you cracked under the weight of the past.

The case takes you through the ruins of a post-revolution city still licking its wounds. You meet union men, strike leaders, drug dealers, ideologues, artists, children with too much knowledge and too little guidance. You see remnants of failed dreams, unfinished revolutions, and memories no one wants to remember. You encounter the Pale—a metaphysical, reality-eroding phenomenon that looms just outside the margins of the known world. And through it all, you are haunted by the shadow of a woman you can’t quite remember, but whose absence is burning a hole in your soul.

Eventually, you learn that the man in the tree—an ex-mercenary named Lely—was killed not as part of a grand political conspiracy, but in a moment of chaotic personal vengeance. A drunk, impulsive act. The killer? A washed-up revolutionary hiding in the ruins of the doomed revolution, rotting with the past. He fired the shot from an abandoned island, and he’s been alone with his memories ever since.

You confront him, and in doing so, you confront the city itself. You confront the weight of history, the futility of ideology, the slow collapse of a world that never lived up to its promises. And just when you think you've seen it all—when you've uncovered every lead, explored every psyche, and come to terms with your own brokenness—you see it. The phasmid. A creature of myth, a massive stick insect long believed to be imaginary, steps out of the reeds and speaks to you through empathic resonance. And it tells you that perhaps you're not beyond redemption.

In the end, you don’t get the girl back. You don’t get your old self back. But maybe that’s okay. You finish the case. You stand up straight. You walk with Kim back to the station, battered, changed, and—just maybe—healing.

Disco Elysium doesn’t end with a bang. It ends with a deep, heavy exhale. A breath after a lifetime of holding it in. It’s not about saving the world—it’s about facing it. And choosing to keep going anyway.

Why do I love Disco Elysium? Because it’s absolutely unhinged—in the smartest, sharpest, most deliberate way possible. It’s a game that throws you headfirst into a world where your brain argues with itself, your thoughts have skill trees, and the line between brilliant philosophy and total nonsense is paper-thin. And somehow, it works. It more than works. It sings.

What makes Disco Elysium so unforgettable for me is its humor—specifically, how it refuses to take anything too seriously, including itself. The writing is absurd, shocking, and brutally funny. It doesn’t matter where you land on the ideological spectrum—this game will tear it apart, point out the flaws, exaggerate them to cartoonish proportions, and then somehow make it meaningful again. You can be a sorry cop, a communist cop, a racist cop, a superstar cop, or a disaster of all of the above. Whatever you choose, the game commits to it. And then it laughs at you, and with you, all at once.

The story itself is impossible to predict. Just when you think you understand where it’s going, it swerves into strange new territory. There’s no formula here, no hero’s journey template. It unfolds organically, shaped by your decisions, your failures, and your willingness to go deeper. And what makes that so powerful is how often you’re rewarded not for winning, but for understanding. Even the dead ends, the breakdowns, the embarrassing choices—they all reveal something real.

The characters are another reason I keep thinking about this game long after the credits rolled. Every single person in Revachol feels layered and alive. Kim Kitsuragi, your partner, is one of the best-written and most quietly profound characters I’ve ever encountered in a game. But it’s not just him. It’s Cuno, the feral child with a twisted mouth and a backstory you don’t see coming. It’s Evrart Claire, the manipulative union boss who’s somehow both despicable and weirdly sincere. It’s Joyce Messier, the intellectual capitalist with a worldview that’s horrifyingly rational. It’s Measurehead, a menacing mountain of supremacist philosophy and taut muscle—both terrifying and strangely pitiful, a man clinging to a worldview he barely understands. And, somehow, it’s also your tie—The Horrific Necktie—a shrieking, flamboyant voice of chaos that may or may not be alive, but definitely has opinions. These aren’t NPCs—they’re people. And talking to them changes you.

There are no clear good guys or bad guys in Disco Elysium. Just people doing their best, or their worst, in a world that forgot how to care. Every choice you make has weight, not because it affects the plot, but because it makes you reflect. This game lives in the moral greyzones—in questions without answers, in systems without justice, in broken people trying to find meaning in a place that’s given up on the idea.

And then there’s the world itself. Revachol is one of the most compelling, fully realized settings I’ve ever seen. Every wall has history. Every conversation reveals another scar. The city feels like it’s mourning something no one can name, and it pulls you in with the quiet, aching gravity of a dream you’re not sure is yours. The lore is dense, strange, political, and deeply personal—and it’s all there if you’re willing to dig.

That’s why I love Disco Elysium. It’s not just that it’s clever or well-written—it’s that it trusts you to meet it on its level. It gives you the freedom to fail, to laugh, to reflect, and to get completely lost in your own mind. It doesn’t hold your hand. It hands you a drink, a thought, a smoldering cigarette, and says, “Well? What are you going to do with all this?”

Disco Elysium is not just a game—it’s an intellectual, emotional, and absurdist trip through the wreckage of ideology, identity, and failure. It’s a miracle of writing, design, and tone management that somehow blends comedy, tragedy, and genuine philosophy into a game where your tie screams at you and your subconscious holds parliamentary debates about your self-worth.

Let’s start with the score: 9/10. It’s not perfect—but what it does, it does so uniquely well that the flaws are just part of its charm. It’s a one-of-a-kind work of interactive storytelling that takes enormous risks, and sticks the landing in almost every direction.

Graphics: 7/10. Disco Elysium isn’t trying to impress you with technical fidelity. The visuals are painterly, loose, and impressionistic—like the world is being remembered through fogged glass or half-faded memory. While it may lack the crisp realism of high-end RPGs, it oozes atmosphere. The environments feel dreamlike and layered, colored with regret and decay. It’s not flashy, but it is memorable. Every place has a mood, and that mood seeps into your choices.

Story: 10/10. This is where the game becomes something else entirely. The story isn’t just a murder mystery—it’s a slow, spiraling journey into the heart of a city, a man, and a world trying to remember why it was worth saving. It’s unpredictable, full of sharp turns and deep reflection. It takes on political theory, philosophy, post-trauma psychology, and still makes room for slapstick and surrealism. And despite all the cynicism, it finds something meaningful in the wreckage. The ending doesn’t give you closure—it gives you something more honest: a sense that moving forward is possible, even if nothing gets fixed.

Gameplay: 6/10. There’s no combat, no crafting, no looting—just conversations, choices, and the constant tug-of-war between the voices in your head. Your stats are personalities. Your skills argue with you. And the game lets you build the detective you want to be—whether that’s a genius, a clown, a wreck, a revolutionary, or a strange cocktail of all four. It’s one of the few games where “failing” skill checks doesn’t end your progress—it just opens a different path. The only reason it doesn’t get a full 10 is the occasional clunky interface moment or bug. But honestly? Those hiccups barely register against the richness of the experience.

The worldbuilding deserves its own category. Revachol isn’t just a setting—it’s a history book written in graffiti, broken flags, and half-finished thoughts. The lore is dense but elegant, layered with real emotion and biting critique. You can spend hours just talking—to people, to buildings, to yourself—and you’ll come away feeling like you’ve learned something. Maybe not something useful. But something true.

And the soundtrack—oh, the soundtrack. Composed by British Sea Power, it’s haunting, aching, defiant. It’s melancholy dressed in elegance. It drifts in and out exactly when it needs to, lifting you in quiet moments or breaking your heart when you finally remember who you are. It’s a companion just as much as Kim.

What makes Disco Elysium special, more than anything, is that it lets you be a mess. It doesn’t ask you to save the world. It just asks you to survive it, understand it, and maybe find some meaning in the chaos. And it gives you the freedom to try—even if you fail spectacularly.

This is a game that challenges how we think about role-playing. About narrative. About games as a medium for exploring identity, systems, and absurdity. It’s not for everyone—but for those who click with it, it’s unforgettable.

Final verdict: bold, broken, brilliant. The kind of game that will sit in the back of your head for years, whispering strange, beautiful thoughts.

Score: 9/10.
